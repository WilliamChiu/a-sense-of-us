Okanogan County, Washington

From 2000 to 2010, the number of people living in households has changed by 1.2893677%. This change is a drastic departure from that of the nation. Data from this state suggests that the region's economic circumstance as well as other variables helped to increase the number of household-population.

In 2000, 5384 people in Okanogan County, Washington lived in nonfamily households.

In 2000, 33329 people in Okanogan County, Washington lived in family households.

In 2000, 851 people in Okanogan County, Washington lived in group quarters.

In 2000, 39564 people in Okanogan County, Washington lived in the county.

In 2010, 1553 people in Lincoln County, Washington lived in nonfamily households.

In 2010, 8923 people in Lincoln County, Washington lived in family households.

In 2010, 94 people in Lincoln County, Washington lived in group quarters.

In 2010, 10570 people in Lincoln County, Washington lived in the county.

Several hundreds of years before Europeans arrived, the area that became Okanogan County was home to numerous indigenous peoples that would eventually become part of three Indian reservations referred to as the Northern Okanogans or Sinkaietk, Tokoratums, Kartars and Konkonelps. They spoke in seven types of Interior Salish languages related to the Puget Sound tribes. The Okanogans experienced a favorable climate, having camped in the winter, hunting bears in the spring, catch fish in the summer and hunt deer in fall. The camps consisted of teepee-like longhouses built with hides and bark. A popular destination for this was the Kettle Falls also situated in Washington where the Columbia River dropped over 20 ft. Meanwhile, women gathered several pieces of nuts, roots and berries.
