Washington County, Tennessee

From 2000 to 2010, the number of people living in households has changed by 3.0627608%. This change is a drastic departure from that of the nation. Data from this state suggests that the region's economic circumstance as well as other variables helped to increase the number of household-population.

In 2000, 17751 people in Washington County, Tennessee lived in nonfamily households.

In 2000, 85434 people in Washington County, Tennessee lived in family households.

In 2000, 4013 people in Washington County, Tennessee lived in group quarters.

In 2000, 107198 people in Washington County, Tennessee lived in the county.

In 2010, 2348 people in Union County, Tennessee lived in nonfamily households.

In 2010, 16609 people in Union County, Tennessee lived in family households.

In 2010, 152 people in Union County, Tennessee lived in group quarters.

In 2010, 19109 people in Union County, Tennessee lived in the county.

Washington County is rooted in the Watauga settlements, which were established in the early 1770s in the vicinity of what is now Elizabethton, in adjacent Carter County. At the outbreak of the Revolutionary War in 1776, the Wataugans organized the "Washington District," which was governed by a committee of safety. North Carolina initially refused to recognize the settlements as legal, but finally agreed to annex the district after the settlers thwarted an invasion by hostile Cherokees. The settlements were governed as the Washington District, which originally included all of what is now Tennessee. The district was reorganized as Washington County in 1777.
