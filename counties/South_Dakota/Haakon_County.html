Haakon County, South Dakota

From 2000 to 2010, the number of people living in households has changed by -19.221252%. This change is a drastic departure from that of the nation. Data from this state suggests that the region suffered stagnated household-population growth perhaps as a result of regional economic issues.

In 2000, 280 people in Haakon County, South Dakota lived in nonfamily households.

In 2000, 1867 people in Haakon County, South Dakota lived in family households.

In 2000, 49 people in Haakon County, South Dakota lived in group quarters.

In 2000, 2196 people in Haakon County, South Dakota lived in the county.

In 2010, 363 people in Faulk County, South Dakota lived in nonfamily households.

In 2010, 1504 people in Faulk County, South Dakota lived in family households.

In 2010, 497 people in Faulk County, South Dakota lived in group quarters.

In 2010, 2364 people in Faulk County, South Dakota lived in the county.

The county was created in 1914 and organized in 1915, and was formed from the original counties of Nowlin and most of Sterling, which had previously been absorbed by Stanley County. It is named for Haakon VII, who became king of Norway in 1905. It is the only county in South Dakota named for a non-American person and is one of only nine counties in South Dakota named for persons who did not live in South Dakota (Clay, Custer, Douglas, Grant, Hamlin, Lincoln, McPherson, and Meade are the other eight). Most of the 66 South Dakota counties are named for early South Dakota officials or legislators, or for physical features (Fall River and Lake), or are derived from Indian words (Minnehaha and Yankton), or from counties in other states (Jones and Walworth), with one (Aurora) named for a Roman goddess, one for an animal (Buffalo), and one (Union) for a concept.
