Ohio County, Kentucky

From 2000 to 2010, the number of people living in households has changed by -4.781103%. This change is a drastic departure from that of the nation. Data from this state suggests that the region suffered stagnated household-population growth perhaps as a result of regional economic issues.

In 2000, 2632 people in Ohio County, Kentucky lived in nonfamily households.

In 2000, 19944 people in Ohio County, Kentucky lived in family households.

In 2000, 340 people in Ohio County, Kentucky lived in group quarters.

In 2000, 22916 people in Ohio County, Kentucky lived in the county.

In 2010, 4058 people in Muhlenberg County, Kentucky lived in nonfamily households.

In 2010, 25490 people in Muhlenberg County, Kentucky lived in family households.

In 2010, 1951 people in Muhlenberg County, Kentucky lived in group quarters.

In 2010, 31499 people in Muhlenberg County, Kentucky lived in the county.

Ohio County was formed in 1798 from land given by Hardin County. Ohio was the 35th Kentucky county in order of formation. It was named for the Ohio River, which originally formed its northern boundary, but lost its northern portions in 1829, when Daviess County and Hancock County were formed. The first settlements in Ohio County were Barnetts Station and Hartford. In 1865, during the American Civil War, the courthouse in Hartford was burned by Kentucky Confederate cavalry because it was being used to house soldiers of the occupying Union Army. The county records were removed first and preserved. Ohio County is famous for its coal mines, which in the 1970s produced much of the nation's coal.
