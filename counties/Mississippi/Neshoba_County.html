Neshoba County, Mississippi

From 2000 to 2010, the number of people living in households has changed by -2.612114%. This change is a drastic departure from that of the nation. Data from this state suggests that the region suffered stagnated household-population growth perhaps as a result of regional economic issues.

In 2000, 3350 people in Neshoba County, Mississippi lived in nonfamily households.

In 2000, 24726 people in Neshoba County, Mississippi lived in family households.

In 2000, 608 people in Neshoba County, Mississippi lived in group quarters.

In 2000, 28684 people in Neshoba County, Mississippi lived in the county.

In 2010, 4522 people in Marshall County, Mississippi lived in nonfamily households.

In 2010, 30885 people in Marshall County, Mississippi lived in family households.

In 2010, 1737 people in Marshall County, Mississippi lived in group quarters.

In 2010, 37144 people in Marshall County, Mississippi lived in the county.

Neshoba County is known as the site of one of the most infamous race-related crimes in American history. In 1964, three civil rights workers were murdered brutally by white supremacists, allegedly including a deputy county sheriff, in Philadelphia, the county seat. The crime and decades-long legal aftermath inspired the 1988 movie ''Mississippi Burning''.
