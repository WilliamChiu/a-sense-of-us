Cherokee County, Alabama

From 2000 to 2010, the number of people living in households has changed by 0.39075613%. This change is reasionably similar to that of the nation. Data from this state suggests that the region's economic circumstance as well as other variables helped to increase the number of household-population.

In 2000, 2743 people in Cherokee County, Alabama lived in nonfamily households.

In 2000, 20885 people in Cherokee County, Alabama lived in family households.

In 2000, 360 people in Cherokee County, Alabama lived in group quarters.

In 2000, 23988 people in Cherokee County, Alabama lived in the county.

In 2010, 3565 people in Cherokee County, Alabama lived in nonfamily households.

In 2010, 22134 people in Cherokee County, Alabama lived in family households.

In 2010, 290 people in Cherokee County, Alabama lived in group quarters.

In 2010, 25989 people in Cherokee County, Alabama lived in the county.

The area included in today's Cherokee County for centuries had belonged to the Cherokee Nation of Native Americans. On December 29, 1835, however, Cherokee leaders signed the controversial Treaty of New Echota, agreeing to surrender their lands in return for new lands west of the Mississippi River. This treaty contributed to the apparent theft of land from the Native Americans by the U.S. government. To this day, there are few Native Americans in Cherokee County.
