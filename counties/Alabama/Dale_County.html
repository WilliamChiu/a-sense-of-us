Dale County, Alabama

From 2000 to 2010, the number of people living in households has changed by 1.6190648%. This change is a drastic departure from that of the nation. Data from this state suggests that the region's economic circumstance as well as other variables helped to increase the number of household-population.

In 2000, 6038 people in Dale County, Alabama lived in nonfamily households.

In 2000, 41426 people in Dale County, Alabama lived in family households.

In 2000, 1665 people in Dale County, Alabama lived in group quarters.

In 2000, 49129 people in Dale County, Alabama lived in the county.

In 2010, 7435 people in Dale County, Alabama lived in nonfamily households.

In 2010, 41899 people in Dale County, Alabama lived in family households.

In 2010, 917 people in Dale County, Alabama lived in group quarters.

In 2010, 50251 people in Dale County, Alabama lived in the county.

The area now known as Dale County was originally inhabited by members of the Creek Indian nation, who occupied all of southeastern Alabama during this period. Between the years of 1764 and 1783 this region fell under the jurisdiction of the colony of British West Florida. The county, together with the surrounding area, was ceded to the United States in the 1814 Treaty of Fort Jackson, ending the Creek Indian Wars. A blockhouse had been constructed during the conflict on the northwestern side of the Choctawhatchee River, and the first non-Indian residents of Dale County would be veterans who began to settle in the area around 1820.
